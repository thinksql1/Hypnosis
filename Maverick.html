Deconstructing "Addiction"

The first and most critical step is to reframe what "addiction" actually is. The world, and the traditional therapy industry, has created a "big scary thing" around the word, which paralyzes both clients and therapists. I operate from a simple, powerful premise:

"There is no such thing as addiction. It's just a problem that someone has in their mind that's causing them to suffer and they've labeled addiction on it."

This perspective is liberating. It means heroin addiction, anxiety, depression, a phobia of spiders... they're all the same at their core. No problem is bigger or smaller than another. This removes your fear as a practitioner and allows you to use a consistent therapeutic approach for any issue a client brings you. You stop seeing a "heroin addict" and start seeing a person with a problem in their mind that needs solving.

Trauma is the Root

Every addiction I've ever encountered comes down to some form of unreleased trauma. But we must also redefine "trauma." It doesn't have to be a catastrophic event. Trauma is any unpleasant memory or life event that causes emotions and memories to become stuck together in a non-supportive way, keeping a person stuck in the past.

Actionable Step:

Mentally discard the labels. When a client tells you their problem, internally translate "I have a crippling gambling addiction" to "This person has a problem in their mind causing suffering." This single shift will change your entire approach from one of fear or specialization to one of universal problem-solving.

The "Different Than" Philosophy

In a market saturated with therapists who all look and sound the same, you must intentionally stand out. I adopted this philosophy from former wrestling promoter Eric Bischoff: you can be "more than, less than, or different than." I choose to be "different than."

My tattoos, my fast speech, my direct language—it's all a conscious choice to be the opposite of every other "professional" a client has seen. This unconventional persona breaks through their preconceived notions of therapy. Clients who have been judged by men in suits see me and feel they won't be judged. My being "straight edge" (never smoked, drank, or did drugs) further shatters their expectations.

This isn't about being a rebel for the sake of it. It's a strategic positioning that attracts clients who are fed up with conventional methods. An early client judged me for my appearance, and after I helped her quit smoking, I realized the importance of letting clients see and hear me *before* they commit. This led to creating over 1,600 videos.

Actionable Step:

Identify the standard persona in your field. Now, list three ways you can be authentically "different than" that standard. This could be in your appearance, your communication style, your session structure, or your marketing. Own that space.

Radical Honesty & Rapport

Traditional therapy is obsessed with building "conscious rapport"—being agreeable, nodding, and creating a safe, polite space. I do the opposite. I consciously break conscious rapport to build a much deeper, more powerful unconscious trust.

"If they do some dumbass s***, I tell my clients, 'that was some dumbass s*** that you did there. You've just wasted your money.'"

Clients are drowning in bullshit—from themselves, from society, from other therapists. Your job is to be the one person who is radically honest with them. This means using blunt language and directly challenging their stories. When you're willing to be that direct, the client's unconscious mind recognizes you as a safe and honest guide. When they start swearing *with you*, you know you've established that deep rapport. It cuts through the victim narrative and forces them to confront their own role in their problem.

Admit Your Judgements

I openly tell clients I judge them. "Oh, by the way, I did judge you. The same thing I did with you, I do with them." Everyone judges. By admitting it, you disarm the concept and show that your initial judgment is irrelevant to your ability to help them.

Actionable Step:

In your next session, find one opportunity to be more direct than you normally would. Challenge a client's statement gently but firmly. Ditch the therapeutic jargon. Speak like a real person. Notice how it changes the dynamic.

100% Client Ownership

This is the endgame. The entire process is designed to return full ownership of the change back to the client. While they may initially put you on a pedestal as the "magnificent fixer," your final and most important job is to kick that pedestal out from under them.

"That change you made? It had absolutely nothing to do with me. It was you. I just worked like a mentor or a trainer."

If they leave thinking you "fixed" them, they are still powerless. They will be dependent on you or another "fixer" for future problems. But if they leave knowing *they* did it, they internalize that power. They gain the self-efficacy to solve other problems in their life—leaving a bad relationship, starting a business, saving money. This is the difference between temporary relief and lasting transformation.

Actionable Step:

At the end of a successful session or program, explicitly reframe the client's success. Use language like: "I was just your guide. You did all the work. You walked the path. This power was in you the whole time." Make it a non-negotiable part of your closing process.

The Art of the Screening Call

The free one-hour screening call is the most important part of my business process. Its purpose is NOT to sell my services. The purpose is to determine if the client is ready and to make them sell themselves to me.

"My goal is to lovingly with respect just try and push them away. Push them away and have them fight for me and the solution."

I do this by asking one powerful question and then shutting up: "After all the things you've tried—rehab, other therapists, NA—why do you think I'm the one you're going to work with?" Then I wait. The silence forces them to articulate their motivation, to take ownership of their desire for change. If they can't sell me on why I should help them, they aren't ready.

Prerequisites for the Call:

  • They must have watched 3-5 of my videos. If they haven't invested 30 minutes to understand my approach, I won't invest an hour in them.
  • They cannot be actively "shopping around." If they are speaking to other practitioners, I tell them to finish those consultations first and call me back if they're still interested. I do not compete.

Actionable Step:

Reframe your consultation from a "sales call" to a "screening call." Your goal is to disqualify, not to convince. Implement the "Why me?" question and become comfortable with the silence that follows.

Spotting Red Flags

During the screening call, you are a detective looking for signs of a client who is not ready for change. These are immediate disqualifiers or require serious reframing:

  • "You're my last hope." This is a massive red flag. They are placing all responsibility on you. If it doesn't work, it will be your fault. This is an abdication of ownership.
  • Overly complimentary / "Buttering you up". This is often a manipulation tactic. They are trying to get you on their side so you'll buy into their story.
  • Attending Rehab. This is a statistical red flag. I tell clients honestly: "The success rate of any therapy drops by 60 to 65% after one stint of rehab... it drops to 10% after two rehabs." Their belief filters are often set to failure. I celebrate when a client *hasn't* been to rehab.
  • Active in AA/NA online groups. These can be breeding grounds for victim mentality ("at effect" thinking). I require clients to leave them during our work together to focus on an "at cause" mindset.

Actionable Step:

Create your own checklist of red flags based on these examples. Be prepared to politely and respectfully decline to work with a client who exhibits them. Saying "no" to the wrong client allows you to say "yes" to the right one and protects your effectiveness.

Pricing & Value Framing

I charge a lot of money. A grand for one session, 2.5 grand for three. I'm "bloody expensive," and I don't work for less. You must be unapologetic about your pricing, but you must also be a master at framing its value.

The price is never mentioned until the end of the screening call, after the value has been established. The key is to frame your fee against the enormous financial and personal cost of their problem.

"I strategically frame the cost by discussing how much money clients spend annually on their addiction. By highlighting these exorbitant costs, I set a context where my fees, while high, are a rational investment for overcoming a problem that's already draining their finances."

Example Costs to Frame Against:

  • Smoking: Can be $35,000 a year.
  • Cocaine: I had a client spending $200,000 a year on himself and his wife.
  • Rehab: One client's mother-in-law spent $300,000 on three rehab stints that failed instantly.

When you put your $2,500 fee next to those numbers, it becomes "cheap." You are not selling your time; you are selling a result that saves them a fortune.

Actionable Step:

Never state your price without first establishing the cost of the client's problem. Ask them directly: "How much is this problem costing you per year, financially, emotionally, and in your relationships?" Anchor their mind to that massive cost before you present your fee as the comparatively small investment.

The Three Invitations

These are three foundational frames presented to every client to set expectations and prime them for success before any formal "hypnosis" begins. They are non-negotiable.

Invitation 1: Be Present and Engage Actively

Redefine hypnosis from a passive experience to an active one. The client is not a passenger; they are the driver.

"This isn't me waving a watch and you're magically fixed. This is like being on a rollercoaster, not watching a movie of a rollercoaster. You have to be in it. See it, hear it, feel it to the best of your ability."

Invitation 2: Listen to Hear – Not Just to Respond

This is designed to bypass the client's critical, analytical mind. Most people listen with the intent to analyze and judge. This is a barrier to unconscious communication.

"I want you to listen in the same way you listen to your favorite relaxing, chill out music. You don't try to figure out the chord structure. You just let it wash over you. I am not talking to *you* (the conscious mind). I am talking to the part of you where the problem is."

Invitation 3: Shelf Everything You Think You Know

The client comes with a library of failed ideas and beliefs. If that knowledge was going to solve their problem, they wouldn't be in your office.

"Put everything you know about your problem, about addiction, about hypnotherapy, on a shelf... because if what you knew was going to work, you would have fixed this by now."

This creates a "whiteboard" mentality, making them open to new information. I also warn them about their internal critical voice, framing it as the unconscious "closing the door."

Actionable Step:

Memorize and internalize these three invitations. Make them a mandatory part of the preamble to every single client session. Do not start the "work" until you have set these frames.

The "Dot-to-Dot" Story

Before any technique, I tell a personal story to frame my role. It's about being five years old, struggling with a dot-to-dot puzzle, and my grandmother refusing to do it for me, saying "No, Luke, I can't do this for you cuz if I do this for you, you'll never learn."

"My job is not to do this for you. My job is to help you make the connections in your brain to achieve your own freedom."

This story is a powerful framing device that accomplishes several critical things at once:

  1. It puts responsibility for "making the connections" squarely on the client.
  2. It defines my role as a guide, not a "fixer" or "savior."
  3. It gives me permission to do literally anything—tell stories, tapping, use direct suggestion, be provocative—and call it "hypnosis," because I've framed all of it as simply helping them find the next dot.

This frame gives you immense flexibility. You are no longer bound by a rigid definition of hypnosis.

Actionable Step:

Develop your own "framing story" that illustrates your role as a guide. It doesn't have to be this one, but it should be personal and clearly communicate that the client is responsible for their own connections and that you are there to facilitate, not "do."

Therapeutic "Stage Hypnosis"

I deliberately integrate "stage stuff"—magnetic fingers, stuck hands, body sways—into serious therapeutic sessions. This is not for entertainment; it's a strategic tool for building belief.

Clients come in with an expectation that hypnosis is "magic." By demonstrating these phenomena, you meet that expectation and provide tangible proof of the power of their mind. You show them, in real-time, that words can create an undeniable physical response.

"If I can make one finger with my suggestion come towards the other finger just through words, then my words bringing sobriety to you... will be far more effective."

After a successful phenomenon, you "milk" it by asking, "Do you see how powerful your mind is? Do you see how powerful hypnosis is going to be for you?" This creates a direct, unconscious bridge between a "silly" magnetic finger trick and the very serious outcome of overcoming their addiction. You are leveraging their belief in the phenomenon to build their belief in the solution.

Actionable Step:

Learn a few simple, reliable hypnotic phenomena. Practice them until you can deliver them with confidence. Plan to use one in every first session as a "convincer" and a belief-building exercise before you address the main problem.

Hypnosis is "Whatever Works"

Once you've framed your role with the "Dot-to-Dot" story, you have unshackled yourself from any specific technique or rigid definition of hypnosis. For the client, hypnosis is now "whatever the hell I want it to be."

This gives you immense freedom. You can use storytelling, tapping, provocative therapy, direct suggestion, indirect metaphor, shamanistic principles—it doesn't matter. It's all just "hypnosis" because you've framed it as such. You are no longer a technician following a script; you are a problem-solver using a flexible toolkit.

"I have no f****** idea how I'm going to work with you... because you each have your own beliefs, your own trauma... unique to you."

This allows you to be "married to what works," not to a specific methodology. You can listen to the client, understand "how they work," and tailor your intervention in real-time. I might add a brief, formal "hypnosis bit" at the end of a session as a "garnish" or "bookend" just to satisfy the client's expectation of what it "looks like," but the real work has already been done.

Actionable Step:

Expand your toolkit. Learn techniques from different modalities (NLP, EFT, storytelling, etc.). Stop thinking "I have to do a formal induction." Instead, think "What does this client need right now to find the next dot?" and use the tool that best fits, knowing you've already framed it all as "hypnosis."

Boundaries & First Aid

While we deal with the mental and emotional aspects, we must be crystal clear about our professional boundaries, especially with severe physiological addictions.

"I am not a medical doctor. For addictions with severe physiological withdrawal risks (e.g., heroin, severe alcohol dependency), clients must consult their physician. I only address the emotional and mental aspects."

Never advise a client to stop a physically addictive substance "cold turkey." Your role is to handle the trauma, the cravings, and the mental patterns. The physical tapering is a medical issue. This disclaimer protects the client and protects you.

Emergency Mental First Aid

Empower clients with practical, discreet tools they can use in the moment when they feel triggered. These are "emergency mental first aid" techniques they can use anywhere.

  • Tapping (EFT)
  • Heart Breathing
  • Simple Finger Holds

Teaching these tools gives the client an immediate sense of control and a practical way to manage triggers while the deeper unconscious work integrates.

Actionable Step:

Create a clear medical disclaimer and use it with every client dealing with physical addiction. Learn at least two simple "pattern interrupt" or state-change techniques you can teach clients as "mental first aid" in their first session.
